One of the most successful methods used by NASA to discover exoplanets is the transit method. This approach involves observing the slight dimming of a star’s light when a planet passes (or "transits") in front of it. NASA’s Kepler Space Telescope was a trailblazer in using this method, and it discovered thousands of exoplanets.

  • 1.What is Exoplanet?Imagine floating in the middle of a vast, dark ocean, where each twinkling star represents a distant island. Some of these islands might harbor secret worlds of their own—planets unlike anything we've seen in our solar system. These hidden realms are known as exoplanets, or extrasolar planets. An exoplanet is a planet that orbits a star outside our solar system, challenging our previous understanding of the universe. While most exoplanets orbit stars, some drift freely in the space between the stars, like cosmic nomads. Their discovery presents tantalizing possibilities—perhaps even the potential for life beyond Earth—sparking a thrilling new chapter in our quest to explore the cosmos.
  • 2.Types of ExoplanetsScientists have identified a rich variety of exoplanets, each with unique characteristics. These planets challenge our understanding of planetary systems and reveal the diversity that exists beyond our solar system.
  • 1.Gas GiantsThese massive planets are primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, much like Jupiter and Saturn. Gas giants don’t have solid surfaces; instead, they consist of thick layers of swirling gases above a dense core.
  • Interesting FactUnlike Jupiter in our solar system, many gas giants in other planetary systems orbit extremely close to their stars. These are called "Hot Jupiters." Their proximity means that their atmospheres can reach scorching temperatures, sometimes thousands of degrees Celsius.
  • 2.Neptunian ExoplanetsThese planets are similar in size to Neptune and Uranus, typically about four times larger than Earth. With atmospheres dominated by hydrogen and helium, Neptunian exoplanets may also contain a mixture of rocky materials in their cores.
  • Interesting FactsMini Neptunes: Some Neptunian exoplanets are smaller and less massive than Neptune itself, often referred to as "mini Neptunes." These planets might have atmospheres that range from thin gas layers to thick clouds, and their exact compositions remain mysterious.
  • Comet-Like TailsWarm Neptunian exoplanets can have enormous, comet-like clouds of gas that are being stripped away from their atmospheres due to intense radiation from their stars. These tails can extend millions of kilometers into space
  • Icy and Far AwayThe first ice giant exoplanet ever discovered lies about 25,000 light-years from Earth—so distant and cold that its icy composition hints at a far more complex planetary environment than expected.
  • 3.Super-EarthsA class of planets larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune, Super-Earths vary greatly in their composition. Some are rocky, while others have thick gaseous atmospheres. Despite their name, Super-Earths do not necessarily resemble Earth, but their size and variety make them an intriguing category, with some scientists speculating they could host conditions suitable for life.
  • Interesting FactsPotential for Life: Super-Earths are particularly intriguing because they fall within a size range that could potentially support life. Some super-Earths lie within their star’s habitable zone, meaning they could have conditions suitable for liquid water.
  • Varide SurfaceVaried Surfaces: While some super-Earths are purely rocky, others might have vast oceans or thick atmospheres. This variety makes them one of the most exciting types of exoplanets to study in the search for habitable worlds.
  • Massive MountainsMassive Mountains: On rocky super-Earths, the gravity is much stronger than on Earth. This means that mountains and land formations would be smaller and denser than on Earth due to the extra force pulling everything downward
  • 4.Terrestrial (Rocky) PlanetsSimilar to Earth, Mars, Venus, and Mercury in our solar system, terrestrial exoplanets have solid surfaces composed primarily of rock and metal. These planets are the ones most likely to be considered for habitability studies, as their rocky surfaces could support liquid water under the right conditions.
  • Closest Earth-Like ExoplanetProxima Centauri b, discovered in 2016, is one of the closest potentially habitable exoplanets. It orbits the star Proxima Centauri, which is just over four light-years away from Earth, sparking interest in the possibility of life outside our solar system.
  • Varying Day LengthsSome terrestrial exoplanets are tidally locked, meaning the same side always faces their star. This creates extreme conditions where one half of the planet might be in constant daylight, while the other is in perpetual darkness.
  • Iron PlanetsSome terrestrial exoplanets, called iron planets, are thought to be composed almost entirely of metal. These dense worlds would have super-hot surfaces and be geologically active.
